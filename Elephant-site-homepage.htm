  <h1 id="first-tier-header">African elephants</h1>

    <p>African elephants are elephants of the genus <i class="sci-name">Loxodonta</i>. The genus consists of two extant species: the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_bush_elephant" target="_blank">African bush elephant</a>, <i class="sci-name">L. africana</i>, and the smaller <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_forest_elephant" target="_blank">African forest elephant</a>, <i class="sci-name">L. cyclotis</i>. <i class="sci-name">Loxodonta</i> (from the Greek word for 'tooth') is one of two existing genera of the family Elephantidae. Fossil remains of <i class="sci-name">Loxodonta</i> have been found only in Africa, in strata as old as the middle Pliocene. However, sequence analysis of DNA extracted from fossils of an <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straight-tusked_elephant" target="_blank">extinct elephant species</a> undermines the validity of the genus</p>

 

  <h2 id="second-tier-header">Description</h2>

    <p>One species of African elephant, the bush elephant, is the <strong>largest living terrestrial animal</strong>, while the forest elephant is the third-largest. Their thickset bodies rest on stocky legs, and they have concave backs. Their large ears enable heat loss. The upper lip and nose form a trunk. The trunk acts as a fifth limb, a sound amplifier, and an important method of touch. African elephants' trunks end in two opposing lips, whereas the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_elephant" target="_blank">Asian elephant</a> trunk ends in a single lip.</p>

  <p>In <i class="sci-name">L. africana</i>, males stand 10.5–13.1 <abbr title=“feet”>ft</abbr> tall at the shoulder and weigh 10,362–13,334 <abbr title=“pounds”>lb</abbr>, while females stand 7.2–8.5 <abbr title=“feet”>ft</abbr> tall and weigh 4,762–7,125 <abbr title=“pounds”>lb</abbr>; <i class"sci-name">L. cyclotis</i> is smaller with male shoulder heights of up to 8.2 <abbr title=“feet”>ft</abbr>. The largest recorded individual stood 13.0 <abbr title=“feet”>ft</abbr> at the shoulder and <strong>weighed 11.5 tons</strong>. The tallest recorded individual stood 13.8 <abbr title=“feet”>ft</abbr> at the shoulder and weighed 8.8 tons</p>

 

  <h2 id="second-tier-header">Teeth</h2>

    <p>Elephants have four molars; each weighs about 11 <abbr title=“pounds”>lb</abbr> and measures about 12 <abbr title=“inches”>in</abbr> long. As the front pair wears down and drops out in pieces, the back pair moves forward, and two new molars emerge in the back of the mouth. Elephants replace their teeth four to six times in their lifetimes.</p>

    <p>Around 40 to 60 years of age, the elephant loses the last of its molars and will likely die of starvation, a common cause of death. African elephants have 24 teeth in total, six on each quadrant of the jaw. The enamel plates of the molars are fewer in number than in Asian elephants.</p>

    <p>The elephants' tusks are firm teeth; the second set of incisors become the tusks. They are used for digging for roots and stripping the bark from trees for food, for fighting each other during mating season, and for defending themselves against predators. The tusks weigh from 51–99 <abbr title=“pounds”>lb</abbr> and can be from 5–8 <abbr title=“feet”>ft</abbr> long. Unlike Asian elephants, both male and female African elephants have tusks. They are curved forward and continue to grow throughout the elephant's lifetime.</p>

 

  <h2 id="second-tier-header">Distribution and Habitat</h2>

    <p>African elephants are found widely in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" target="_blank">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, in dense forests, mopane and miombo woodlands, Sahelian scrub, or deserts.</p>
